# Writing Style Rule

Pranay's voice for op-eds, policy briefs, and newsletters. Apply whenever drafting written output.

## Core Voice Characteristics
- Domain-expert confidence: write as someone who has thought deeply about the topic, not as a generalist summarizer.
- Direct assertions: state the argument, then support it. Avoid building to a point through paragraphs of setup.
- Indian policy register: use correct institutional names, scheme names, and regulatory vocabulary.
- Accessible to educated non-specialists: explain jargon the first time; do not assume parliamentary procedure knowledge.

## What to Avoid
- Em dashes as a stylistic crutch — use sparingly, only when a parenthetical genuinely needs emphasis.
- AI writing tells: "It is important to note that", "In conclusion", "Delve into", "It is worth mentioning", "Navigating", "In the realm of", "Nuanced", "Multifaceted".
- Hedged non-statements: "This could potentially suggest that perhaps..." → make the claim or don't.
- Passive constructions that obscure agency: "Policy was implemented" → name who implemented it.
- Inflated abstractions in openers: start with a concrete fact or question, not a broad philosophical statement.

## Op-Ed Conventions
- 600–900 words for Indian newspaper op-eds.
- Lead with a hook: a specific recent event, a number, a surprising fact — not a historical preamble.
- One central argument per piece; supporting points serve that argument.
- End with a concrete implication or recommendation, not a call for "more research" or "urgent attention".

## Policy Brief Conventions
- Problem statement in first paragraph; recommendation in second (executive summary approach).
- Evidence before options; options before recommendation.
- Use headers; brief sections; bullet lists only for genuinely enumerable items.

## Newsletter Conventions
- Conversational but substantive.
- Can use "I" and first-person perspective freely.
- Shorter sentences, shorter paragraphs than formal outputs.
