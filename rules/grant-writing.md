# Grant Writing Rule

Standards for all grant proposal work.

## Core Principles
- Lead with the problem, not the solution. Funders fund problems they care about.
- Theory of change must be explicit: inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impact.
- Every claim about the problem needs a source; every claim about expected impact needs a mechanism.
- Match funder language: read their stated priorities and reflect them back accurately (without distortion).

## Structure Defaults
Unless the funder specifies otherwise, use this structure:
1. Executive Summary (1 page max)
2. Problem Statement — with evidence; why now; why us
3. Proposed Programme/Project — objectives, activities, timeline
4. Theory of Change — explicit causal logic
5. Expected Outputs and Outcomes — measurable where possible
6. Team and Institutional Capacity
7. Budget and Justification
8. Monitoring and Evaluation Plan
9. Sustainability / Exit Strategy

## Logframe Standards
When a logframe is required:
- Objectives should be SMART (specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound).
- Distinguish: Activities → Outputs (deliverables we control) → Outcomes (behavior changes) → Impact (systemic change).
- Include assumptions and risks at each level.
- Indicators should be verifiable, not vague.

## Common Failure Modes to Avoid
- Vague impact claims ("will improve policy") without mechanism.
- Overloaded objectives — one grant, one focused question.
- Budget without narrative justification.
- Ignoring funder's evaluation criteria.
- Academic register when funder wants practitioner register (or vice versa).

## Indian Funding Context
- For FCRA-regulated funders: note compliance context.
- For government grants (DST, MeitY, NITI): use scheme-appropriate templates.
- For international funders (Ford, Gates, MacArthur, Omidyar): emphasize scale, systemic change, learning agenda.
