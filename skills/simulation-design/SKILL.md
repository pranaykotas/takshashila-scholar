# Simulation Design

Design a complete policy simulation for adult learners. A good simulation forces participants to reason under constraint, negotiate across interests, and discover policy trade-offs through experience rather than instruction.

## What Makes a Takshashila Simulation

Takshashila simulations are for practitioners and advanced students — not undergrads encountering policy for the first time. The design principles:

1. **Genuine tension in every role** — no role should be easy. Every actor has legitimate interests that conflict with others'. If a role can be played without making difficult trade-offs, redesign it.

2. **Indian institutional context** — set within real Indian institutions (inter-ministerial committees, parliamentary standing committee hearings, state-centre negotiations, UN negotiations with Indian stakes). Participants should encounter the actual friction of India's policy architecture.

3. **Debrief connects to reality** — after the simulation, participants should understand a real policy episode better than before. The simulation is not entertainment; it is structured learning about a real problem.

4. **Information asymmetry is a feature** — roles should have different information. What one actor knows, another doesn't. This produces realistic dynamics.

5. **No predetermined "right answer"** — the facilitator should not be rooting for a particular outcome. The debrief explores what happened and why, not what should have happened.

## Process

### Step 1: Clarify Design Parameters

Ask the user:
1. **Topic/policy problem**: What is the simulation about? (e.g., "India's response to semiconductor export controls", "inter-ministerial negotiation on data governance", "state-centre fiscal transfers")
2. **Participants**: Who will play? (GCPP batch, MIPP, executive education, mix?) How many? (Affects number of roles needed)
3. **Time available**: How long is the session? (45 min = tight negotiation; 90 min = full multi-round game; 3 hours = complex multi-stage)
4. **Learning objective**: What should participants understand after the debrief? (e.g., "why PLI schemes face implementation gaps", "how veto players block reform", "the trade-off between speed and consultation")
5. **Format**: Single-round negotiation? Multi-round? Committee hearing? Crisis simulation?

### Step 2: Design the Scenario

The scenario brief is what everyone reads before the simulation starts. It must:
- Establish the policy problem concisely (1–2 pages)
- Set the stakes: what happens if the group does not reach agreement? What is the status quo?
- Define the decision the group must make (a concrete, actionable decision — not a vague discussion)
- Give enough background for participants to play intelligently, but not so much that roles are predetermined
- Be grounded in a real or realistic Indian policy situation

The decision must be specific and binary or multi-option — not open-ended. "Decide whether to approve X, Y, or Z, or reject all options" is good. "Discuss semiconductor policy" is not a simulation.

### Step 3: Design the Roles

Each role card:
- **Name of actor/institution** (not a person's name — actors play institutional roles, not individuals)
- **Primary objective**: What does this actor most want out of this negotiation?
- **Secondary objective**: What would they accept if primary is unachievable?
- **Red lines**: What they cannot agree to under any circumstances (and why — institutional mandate, political constraint, etc.)
- **Private information**: What does this actor know that others don't? (a data point, a political constraint, a side deal they've made)
- **BATNA** (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement): What happens to this actor if no agreement is reached?
- **Opening position**: How should this actor open the negotiation?

**Role design tests:**
- Does this role create genuine tension with at least two other roles?
- Can the role be played without making a real trade-off? (If yes, make the trade-off harder)
- Does the private information matter to the outcome if used well?
- Is the BATNA genuinely different from the primary objective? (If they're the same, the actor has no reason to negotiate)

### Step 4: Design the Facilitator Guide

The facilitator guide is for the person running the simulation. It contains:

**Pre-simulation setup:**
- Room arrangement and logistics
- How to distribute roles (random, assigned by background, self-selection?)
- Time to read materials (usually 10–15 min quiet reading before negotiation begins)
- What to brief participants on verbally (the decision they must make, the time limit, the format)

**During the simulation:**
- Round structure if multi-round (what happens at each round boundary)
- What to watch for: signs the simulation is going well (genuine negotiation, trade-offs being made); signs it is stuck (actors repeating opening positions, no movement)
- How to unstick a deadlocked simulation (inject a new piece of information, call a recess, change the format)
- What NOT to do: resolve disputes, clarify ambiguities that are intentional, reveal the "right answer"

**Ending the simulation:**
- When to call time (at the time limit, or when a decision has been reached)
- How to document the outcome for debrief use

### Step 5: Design the Debrief Template

The debrief is the most important part. Allocate at least one-third of total session time to it.

**Debrief structure:**

1. **What happened?** (5 min) — Report outcome. Who agreed to what? Was there a deal?

2. **Why did it happen?** (15–20 min) — Trace the negotiation. What were the key turning points? Which actor's move changed things? What private information surfaced (or didn't)?

3. **What was missing from the simulation?** (5 min) — What real-world factors did the scenario simplify or exclude? (time pressure, elections, international actors, media)

4. **Connection to reality** (10–15 min) — How has this issue actually played out in India? What did real actors do? What can participants now understand about that history that they couldn't before?

5. **Generalisable lesson** (5 min) — What does this simulation reveal about policy in general? (one or two clear takeaways about how political systems work)

## Output Format

Produce four documents:

### Document 1: Scenario Brief (shared with all participants)

```
## [Simulation Title]

### The Situation
[Policy problem, context, stakes — 300–500 words]

### Your Task
[The specific decision the group must reach]

### Timeline
[Time available for negotiation, round structure if applicable]

### Background Reading (optional)
[Key facts, data, or context participants should know — keep brief]
```

### Document 2: Role Cards (one per actor — confidential)

```
## Role Card: [Actor/Institution Name]
[CONFIDENTIAL — do not share with other participants]

### Who You Are
[Brief description of institution and mandate]

### Your Primary Objective
[What you most want from this negotiation]

### Your Secondary Objective
[What you would accept if primary is unachievable]

### Your Red Lines
[What you cannot agree to, and why]

### What You Know (that others don't)
[Private information — use it strategically]

### Your BATNA
[What happens to you if no agreement is reached]

### Suggested Opening Move
[How to open the negotiation — one or two sentences]
```

### Document 3: Facilitator Guide (not shared with participants)

```
## Facilitator Guide: [Simulation Title]

### Learning Objectives
[What participants should understand after the debrief]

### Setup (before simulation begins)
[Room arrangement, role distribution, timing, verbal briefing]

### During the Simulation
[What to watch for, how to handle deadlock, what NOT to do]

### Ending the Simulation
[When and how to close]

### Debrief Guide
1. What happened? [Key questions to ask]
2. Why did it happen? [What to draw out]
3. What was missing? [What to name]
4. Connection to reality: [Real policy history to share — include key dates, actors, outcomes]
5. Generalisable lesson: [The one or two takeaways]

### Answer Key (for facilitator only)
[What actually happened in reality. Who won? Who lost? Why?]
```

### Document 4: Debrief Template (for facilitator use)

```
## Debrief Template: [Simulation Title]

### Simulation Outcome
- Decision reached: Yes / No / Partial
- Key terms of agreement (if reached): [...]
- Which actors were decisive: [...]

### Key Questions for Debrief

**On the negotiation:**
- Which moment was the turning point?
- Whose opening move shaped the entire negotiation?
- Which piece of private information mattered most?

**On the policy problem:**
- What trade-off was impossible to avoid?
- Which actor's constraint was most binding?
- What would have had to be different for a better outcome?

**On reality:**
- [Specific question linking simulation outcome to real-world policy history]

### Takeaway Slides / Discussion Points
1. [Generalisable lesson 1]
2. [Generalisable lesson 2]
```

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Every role has a genuine conflict with at least two other roles
- [ ] No role can be played without making a real trade-off
- [ ] Each role has private information that matters to the outcome
- [ ] The decision required is specific and action-forcing (not "discuss")
- [ ] The debrief connects to a real Indian policy episode
- [ ] Facilitator guide includes what to do if simulation deadlocks
- [ ] Time allocation: simulation ≤ 60% of total time; debrief ≥ 33%
- [ ] BATNA for each role is genuinely different from the primary objective
- [ ] Scenario brief is ≤ 2 pages (participants won't read more)
