# Facilitator Guide Template

```
## Facilitator Guide: [Simulation Title]
[NOT FOR PARTICIPANT DISTRIBUTION]

### Learning Objectives
By the end of the debrief, participants will:
1. [Specific analytical insight — what they understand about the policy problem that they didn't before]
2. [Specific procedural insight — what they understand about how policy decisions get made]

### Setup (15 min before start)

**Room arrangement:**
[Describe: round tables for group negotiation, or separate breakout spaces for multi-group, or plenary for committee-style]

**Role distribution:**
[How to assign roles: random draw / assigned by facilitator / self-selection / assigned based on participant background]

**What to tell participants before they start reading:**
> "You have [X] minutes to read your role card. Do not share it with other participants. The decision you must reach is [restate the decision from scenario brief]. You will have [Y] minutes to negotiate. I will not intervene to clarify ambiguities — the ambiguity is intentional."

**Timing:**
- Role card reading: [X] min (quiet, individual)
- Negotiation: [Y] min
- Debrief: [Z] min (allocate at least one-third of total time)

### During the Simulation

**Signs it's going well:**
- Actors are making offers and counter-offers (not just repeating opening positions)
- At least one actor has shifted position
- Private information has surfaced (even if not everyone knows it surfaced)
- Real trade-offs are being made (someone is giving something up)

**Signs it's stuck (and what to do):**
- All actors repeating opening positions after 15+ min: Call a 5-minute recess. Tell actors to come back with one concrete offer.
- One actor dominating without challenge: Ask a quieter actor directly: "What would [dominant actor]'s proposal mean for your institution?"
- No agreement seems possible: Do NOT resolve it for them. Let the simulation end in deadlock — that itself is data for the debrief.
- Someone goes off-script or gets personally invested: Remind them they are playing an institutional role, not themselves.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not clarify ambiguities in role cards — the ambiguity is intentional
- Do not suggest solutions or move actors toward agreement
- Do not reveal the "right answer" or what happened in reality during the simulation
- Do not let one actor reveal another's role card

**When to end:**
- At the time limit, regardless of whether agreement was reached
- If agreement is reached early (rare): use the extra time to challenge the agreement ("Are you sure [Actor X] would actually agree to this?")

### Debrief Guide

**1. What happened? (5 min)**
Questions:
- "What was the outcome? Did we reach agreement?"
- "If yes — what exactly was agreed? Read it back."
- "If no — where did it break down?"

**2. Why did it happen? (15–20 min)**
Questions:
- "What was the turning point in the negotiation?"
- "Whose opening move shaped the entire dynamic?"
- "[Name a specific moment]: What happened here? What was [actor] trying to do?"
- "What private information surfaced? How did it change things?"
- "Which actor's constraint was most binding on the outcome?"
- "If you were playing [different role], what would you have done differently?"

**3. What was missing from the simulation? (5 min)**
Tell participants what the simulation simplified or excluded:
- [Time pressure from elections / media / court deadlines]
- [International actor with direct stake]
- [Budget constraint not modelled]
- [Previous relationship between actors not captured]
"How would [missing factor] have changed the outcome?"

**4. Connection to reality (10–15 min)**
[This section is specific to the simulation — fill in with real history]
- What actually happened: [...]
- Who won, who lost, and why: [...]
- What did real actors do that simulation participants didn't (or vice versa): [...]
- Key question: "Now that you know what actually happened — can you see why? What does that tell us about [policy system / institution / political economy]?"

**5. Generalisable lesson (5 min)**
Close with one or two lessons that apply beyond this specific case:
1. [Lesson about how policy decisions get made — general principle]
2. [Lesson about what determines policy outcomes — general principle]

### Answer Key (Facilitator Only)

**What actually happened in reality:**
[Full account of the real policy episode — dates, actors, decisions, outcomes]

**Which role was most powerful and why:**
[...]

**Which private information was most decisive:**
[...]

**Common errors participants make in this simulation:**
[What most groups get wrong, and what that reveals]
```
