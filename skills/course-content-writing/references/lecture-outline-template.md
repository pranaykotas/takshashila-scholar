# Lecture Outline Template

```
## Lecture: [Title]

**Course:** [GCPP / MIPP / Executive / Other]
**Session:** [Session number in course sequence]
**Duration:** [Total minutes]
**Learning objective:** By the end, participants will be able to [specific action — analyse / distinguish / apply / evaluate — NOT "understand" or "know about"]
**Audience:** [Who — their professional background and what they already know]
**Prior knowledge assumed:** [What participants should already know from previous sessions or professional experience. Be specific.]
**What this session challenges:** [The prior assumption or intuition this session will productively disrupt]

---

### Opening Provocation (__ min)

[The question, case, data point, or puzzle that opens the session. Must:
- Create genuine intellectual discomfort — participants should not immediately know the answer
- Be concrete and specific (not "why is policy hard?")
- Be answerable using the concept introduced in this lecture — but not yet

Exact wording to use:
"[Quote the opening question or prompt verbatim]"

Facilitator note: Wait for responses. Do not immediately answer. Use responses to gauge prior knowledge and calibrate depth.]

---

### Core Concept (__ min)

**Concept:** [Name]

**Precise definition:**
> [Definition — not a loose description but a precise, citable definition. If from a specific author, name them.]

**Key distinction — what this concept IS vs. what it IS NOT:**
- It IS: [...]
- It is NOT: [Common misconception to address]

**Origin / intellectual tradition (brief):**
[Where does this concept come from? One or two sentences. Do not over-teach the history — just enough for credibility and intellectual honesty.]

**Why this concept matters for policy:**
[Direct application: what does knowing this concept let you do or see that you couldn't before? One concrete example.]

---

### Application to Indian Case (__ min)

**Case:** [Name — Indian case preferred]

**The case in brief:**
[50–100 words of factual background. Then stop — do not analyse yet.]

**Applying the concept:**
[Walk through the application step by step. Show the analytical work explicitly — do not jump to conclusions.

Step 1: Identify [relevant elements of the concept] in the case
Step 2: Apply [specific dimension of the concept]
Step 3: What does this reveal?

The key question to answer: "What does [concept] illuminate about this case that a factual account alone would not?"]

**What the case reveals about the concept (and vice versa):**
[Mutual illumination: the case should deepen understanding of the concept; the concept should deepen understanding of the case. Name both directions explicitly.]

---

### Complications and Limits (__ min)

**Where the concept breaks down:**
[Under what conditions does the concept fail to apply or give misleading results? Be specific.]

**Key assumptions the concept makes:**
[What must be true for the concept to work? What happens when those assumptions are violated?]

**What the concept misses:**
[What important phenomena does the concept not capture? Do not pretend the concept is complete.]

**The ongoing debate (if any):**
[If scholars disagree about this concept, name the main positions — briefly. Participants should know the intellectual landscape.]

---

### Discussion / Application Exercise (__ min)

**Second case (different from main application):**
[Brief description — 2–3 sentences. Enough for participants to engage; not so much that analysis is done for them.]

**Instruction to participants:**
> "Apply [concept] to this case. [Specific question]. You have [X] minutes to discuss with your neighbour / in small groups."

**Facilitator notes:**
- What to listen for: [signs of correct application / common mistakes]
- What to draw out in plenary: [2–3 key points to surface]
- What to NOT resolve for them: [deliberate ambiguity to preserve]

---

### Close (__ min)

**The one thing:**
[The single insight that connects everything covered in this lecture. Not a summary — a synthetic claim. If participants remember only one thing, what should it be?]

Write it as a complete, memorable sentence:
> "[The one thing]"

**Bridge to next session:**
[One sentence connecting today's concept to the next session — creates intellectual continuity]

---

### Readings

**Required:**
- [Author, Title, Year] — read [sections/pages]. Focus on [specific argument or evidence].

**Recommended (for those who want more):**
- [Author, Title, Year]

**Indian context / data (if applicable):**
- [Specific government report, committee report, or data source relevant to the Indian application]
```
