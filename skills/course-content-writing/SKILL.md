# Course Content Writing

Design teaching material for adult learners in public policy. The goal is not transmission of information — it is development of analytical capacity. Adults with professional experience learn by connecting new concepts to what they already know and by being challenged to apply ideas to real situations.

## Design Principles

**Adult learners are peers.** GCPP and MIPP participants are working professionals, civil servants, journalists, and lawyers. They know things the instructor doesn't. The content should treat them as such — no oversimplification, no "imagine you are a policymaker" condescension.

**One concept, deeply.** A lecture that covers seven ideas covers none. Each lecture should develop one analytical idea clearly enough that participants can apply it to an unfamiliar case.

**Indian examples first.** Generic international examples (used repeatedly in most policy curricula) produce generic thinking. Start with Indian cases: Union Budget, GST Council, DRSC committee functioning, specific PLI schemes, specific judicial interventions. Participants can map from the familiar to the international, not the other way around.

**Discussion questions are generative, not recall.** "What are the three types of market failure?" is a recall question. "Can you think of an Indian sector where two types of market failure apply simultaneously? What does that imply for regulation?" is an analytical question. Write the second kind.

**Assessment should test application, not memorisation.** If the question can be answered by copy-pasting from the lecture notes, it is not a good question.

## Process

### Step 1: Clarify Content Parameters

Ask the user:
1. **Topic**: What concept, problem, or policy area is the content about?
2. **Content type**: Lecture outline / Reading guide / Case study / Discussion questions / Assessment rubric / Full module (combination)?
3. **Audience**: GCPP (generalist policy), MIPP (international), executive education (practitioners), other?
4. **Prior knowledge assumed**: What do participants already know? (Don't re-teach what they know; challenge what they think they know)
5. **Duration**: Lecture? (60, 90 min) Reading? (how much time to allocate?) Session?
6. **Learning objective**: What should participants be able to do after this content that they couldn't do before? (This should be an action: "analyse X", "distinguish between Y and Z", "apply framework W to a new case")

### Step 2: Design the Lecture Outline

A lecture outline is a teaching plan, not a presentation plan. It tells the instructor what to do in what order, not just what to say.

**Structure:**
1. **Opening provocation** (5–10 min): A question, case, data point, or puzzle that makes participants realize they don't know something they thought they did. Sets up the intellectual problem the lecture will solve.
2. **Core concept** (20–30 min): One analytical idea, explained precisely. Define terms. Give the clearest version of the concept. Show where it comes from (briefly).
3. **Application to Indian case** (15–20 min): Apply the concept to one Indian case in depth. Not a summary of the case — use the case to show how the concept illuminates something the case study alone doesn't reveal.
4. **Complications and limits** (10–15 min): Where does the concept break down? What are its assumptions? What does it miss?
5. **Discussion / application exercise** (15–20 min): Give participants a second case (different from the main one) and ask them to apply the concept themselves. This is active learning — not more lecture.
6. **Close** (5 min): The one thing to remember. Not a summary of everything covered — the single insight that connects it all.

### Step 3: Design the Reading Guide

A reading guide helps participants read analytically, not passively. It should have:

**Before reading:**
- The question the reading is trying to answer (1–2 sentences)
- What prior assumption or view the reading challenges
- What to watch for: the key claim, the key evidence, the key weakness

**While reading:**
- 3–5 guiding questions to keep in mind
- Note: which sections are essential vs. skim

**After reading:**
- 2–3 analytical questions (not recall — synthesis and critique)
- Connection question: "How does this reading change (or not change) how you think about [topic from previous session]?"

### Step 4: Design the Case Study

A case study is a structured analytical exercise, not a factual story. It has:

**The setup** (1–2 pages):
- What happened (briefly and factually)
- The decision point: a specific moment where a choice had to be made
- The actors, their interests, the constraints

**The analytical question** (explicit, not hidden):
- What is the participant asked to analyse? State it directly.
- This should be answerable using concepts from the course, not just good judgment.

**Teaching notes** (for instructor only):
- What concept(s) does this case illustrate?
- What are the two or three most important things to draw out in discussion?
- What do most participants get wrong or miss?
- What actually happened (if based on a real case)?

### Step 5: Design Discussion Questions

Discussion questions for policy education fall into types:

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Analysis** | Apply a concept | "Using the principal-agent framework, analyse why MNREGS implementation varies across states" |
| **Comparison** | See difference | "Compare how India and South Korea handled the same semiconductor supply chain risk. What accounts for the difference?" |
| **Critique** | Stress-test | "What is the strongest argument against [the policy we just discussed]? Is it answered in the readings?" |
| **Application** | Transfer | "If you were advising the Finance Secretary on [problem], what would you recommend — and what would you not recommend?" |
| **Synthesis** | Connect ideas | "How do today's readings change (or not change) what we covered last week on [related topic]?" |

Avoid recall questions. Avoid questions with one right answer that can be looked up. Avoid "what do you think?" (too open). Questions should be answerable by a prepared participant in 3–5 minutes of thinking.

### Step 6: Design Assessment Rubric

Assessment rubric structure:

**Task description**: What exactly is being assessed? (assignment, exam question, presentation)

**Criteria** (3–5, not more):
Each criterion should assess a different skill:
- Analytical clarity: Is the argument clear and logically structured?
- Evidence use: Is evidence cited, relevant, and sufficient?
- Application of concepts: Are course concepts used correctly and insightfully?
- Engagement with counterarguments: Are alternatives considered and addressed?
- Indian context: Is the analysis grounded in the Indian institutional context?

**Grading levels** per criterion (4 levels is enough: Excellent / Good / Adequate / Insufficient)

## Output Format

### Lecture Outline

```
## Lecture: [Title]
**Duration:** [X minutes]
**Learning objective:** By the end, participants will be able to [specific action]
**Audience:** [GCPP / MIPP / Executive / Mixed]
**Prior knowledge assumed:** [What participants should already know]

### Opening provocation (__ min)
[The question, case, or puzzle. Exact wording to use.]

### Core concept (__ min)
[Concept name and precise definition]
[Key distinctions: what it is vs. what it is not]
[Origin or intellectual tradition (brief)]

### Indian application (__ min)
[Case name]
[How the concept illuminates the case: step by step]
[What the case reveals that the concept alone doesn't — and vice versa]

### Complications and limits (__ min)
[Where the concept breaks down]
[What assumptions it makes]
[What it misses]

### Discussion exercise (__ min)
[Second case, briefly described]
[Instruction to participants: "Apply [concept] to this case. What does it reveal?"]
[Facilitator notes: what to listen for, what to draw out]

### Close (__ min)
[The one thing: the insight that connects everything covered]

### Reading list
- [Required reading]
- [Optional/advanced]
```

## Reading Guide: [Title of reading]

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Learning objective is an action ("analyse", "distinguish", "apply"), not a topic ("understand semiconductors")
- [ ] Opening provocation creates genuine intellectual discomfort — participants should not know the answer
- [ ] At least one Indian case used (not a generic international example)
- [ ] Discussion questions are analytical, not recall
- [ ] Case study has an explicit analytical question (not hidden in the case)
- [ ] Assessment rubric has 3–5 criteria max; each tests a different skill
- [ ] Nothing in the content condescends to the audience (no "imagine you are a policymaker")
- [ ] Lecture outline has estimated timing for each section
