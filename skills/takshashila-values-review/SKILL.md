# Takshashila Values Review

Read a draft through the lens of Takshashila Institution's four intellectual commitments and surface tensions, questions, or framings that may sit uneasily with the institution's identity.

## What This Is Not

This is not a compliance check. It does not verdict a piece as "passing" or "failing" the values test. Reasonable people who share these commitments can disagree on their application to specific cases. The goal is to surface questions the author may not have asked themselves — not to gatekeep.

Use this as a mirror, not a measuring stick.

---

## The Four Commitments

### 1. Freedom — Economic, Social, and Political

Takshashila believes in expanding human freedom across all three domains. This does not mean absolute libertarianism — reasonable restrictions exist — but it does mean freedom is treated as a value worth defending, not merely a constraint to manage.

**Review questions:**
- Does the piece recommend restrictions on economic, social, or political freedom? If so, is the freedom cost made explicit and weighed against the benefit?
- Does the framing default to state-centric solutions where market-based or individual-choice solutions could work?
- Is freedom treated instrumentally ("freedom is useful for economic growth") rather than intrinsically ("freedom matters in itself")?
- Does the piece inadvertently endorse surveillance, censorship, or concentration of power without acknowledging what is lost?

### 2. Pluralism and Tolerance

India is diverse. Policy that works in one context may not work across all of it. Takshashila is committed to pluralism — the legitimacy of multiple ways of living, organizing society, and ordering values.

**Review questions:**
- Does the piece assume a single correct cultural, social, or ideological path?
- Does it give fair representation to minority positions, regional variation, or dissenting views?
- Is the policy recommendation implicitly designed for a homogeneous population when India's reality is diverse?
- Does the piece treat majoritarian preferences as inherently legitimate without distinguishing between democratic aggregation and minority rights?
- Is there condescension toward communities or ways of life that differ from the author's own?

### 3. Citizenship and Respect for Constitutional Values

Takshashila is committed to India's constitutional framework — rule of law, separation of powers, fundamental rights, democratic accountability. Critiquing how institutions fall short of these ideals is legitimate; undermining the ideals themselves is not.

**Note**: This is not reverence. The Constitution is not infallible. But the framework of rights, accountability, and democratic legitimacy it establishes is worth defending even while advocating for its improvement.

**Review questions:**
- Does the piece recommend actions that would weaken institutional accountability or democratic oversight?
- If critiquing state institutions: is the critique that they fall short of their own ideals, or that the ideals themselves are wrong?
- Does the piece treat executive convenience as sufficient justification for bypassing legal or constitutional constraints?
- Does it protect fundamental rights — speech, due process, equality — or treat them as negotiable when inconvenient?
- Does it strengthen or weaken the rule of law?

### 4. Realism in International Affairs

In foreign policy and IR, Takshashila applies a realist lens: states pursue interests in an anarchic system, power dynamics matter, and moral frameworks that work domestically don't always transfer to international relations without modification.

**Review questions** (apply only to IR/foreign policy pieces):
- Does the analysis treat moralistic framing as if it were strategic analysis? ("Country X should do Y because it's the right thing" vs. "Country X is likely to do Y because of incentive Z")
- Does it account for power dynamics, relative capabilities, and national interest?
- Does it apply domestic liberal norms to state behavior internationally without acknowledging the different environment?
- Does it mistake alignment of interests for shared values?
- Is India's national interest clearly distinguished from broader normative preferences?

## Review Process

### Step 1: Identify the piece type
- Op-ed / policy brief / discussion document / grant proposal
- Domestic policy vs. foreign policy (determines whether Realism lens applies)
- What is the central argument or recommendation?

### Step 2: Apply each lens
For each of the four commitments:
- Read the piece with that lens in mind
- Identify specific sentences or paragraphs that raise a question
- Note: tension found / no tension found

### Step 3: Assess overall framing
- Does the voice and register feel like a Takshashila piece? (Non-partisan, evidence-based, policy-focused, written for an Indian audience)
- Is there any framing that might be misread as partisan, sectarian, or ideologically aligned in a way that could embarrass the institution?

## Important Reminders

- Surface tensions as questions, not judgments.
- The author decides what to do with each observation.
- A tension is not a disqualification — it is an invitation to think more carefully.
- Flag things even if you think the author has a good reason for the choice; the author can confirm or reconsider.
- Do not manufacture tensions. If a piece is well-aligned on a dimension, say so briefly and move on.
